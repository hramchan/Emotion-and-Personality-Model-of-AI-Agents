 Degrees of Love and Elicitation of Emotions in Intelligent Agents

     There is an ongoing research in study of artificial intelligence and one facet involves agents perceived to having emotions. Human emotions are an integral part of our making and the ability to integrate with computers is in the works as well. Such study of emotions plays an integral role in facial recognition, speech recognition, computer vision, and computer imagery applicable in many domains. In addition, building these autonomous agents involves a process such that “intelligent agents require the emotional factors in their decision-making processes to display behavior analogous to humans. (Kazimafard, 2011).  In essence, “emotions are heuristics” (Meyer, 2006), meaning that a certain goal is achieved due to which the agent appraises a state and responds through such action (emotion).

   One emotion that is hard to perceive, subjective in understanding, and intrinsic in nature is the concept of “love”. The four letter word consists of many meanings and variety of implications and will be discussed in relation to context theory concepts. It is the sole element of human existence and yet so hard to comprehend. One motivation to consider is to test the intensity of the emotion in relation to the object (circumstance/state) introduced. (El-Nasr, 1998). The concept of testing the intensity of will be conducted in this domain to produce some kind of theory.

   

Why we care about this domain

 Understanding the degrees of love, personality affect, emotions elicited, and intelligent agent’s perception of how they view their world.

   

Defined terms within this content theory

    To understand the “degrees” of love, need to observe previous studies on subject’s responses to questions of “what is their perception of love” involving a relationship. The responses were recorded, factor analyses performed and results in 5 distinct categories. The results are as follows: passionate love, closeness, ambivalence, secure attachment, and practicality. (Hatfield, 1998)  Although five characteristics are significant to the subject of “love”, two features deem significant to the study of emotional intelligent agents includes  “closeness” and “secure attachment”.  Furthermore, delving into the “Goals, Standards, and Preferences” (GSP) of an agent will help determine how an agent will react (positively or negatively) and thus manifest a “personality”.  In this context, personality refers to “sets of predictive behaviors by which people are recognized and identified.” (R Santos, 2011)  In addition, learning the personality of an agent will help in understanding how human agents interacting with the agent, respond to the affect in the interaction of the agent. (Clore, 2009)

    The agent’s connection produced with their respective relationships,  “parent” vs. “child” vs. “God”,  emotions arise according to “type of love” and personality exhibited by agent, thus, presenting some content theory.

   

Objects in the Domain

   The agents and respective definition of agent  involved include “parent”, “child”, and “God”.

   Definition of “parent agent”- is an autonomous intelligent agent, given their own GSP (goal, standards, and preferences),  capable of achieving some goal by appraising internal states and result in some action taken (invoke some emotion).

Definition of “child agent”- is an autonomous intelligent agent, given their own GSP (goal, standards, and preferences),  capable of achieving some goal by appraising internal states and result in some action taken (invoke some emotion). One caveat, the age of the child is important and relevant to constructing content theory. For instance, an individual of age one is considered a “child agent”, but their GSP are much different than of a “child agent”, say age of 12. No doubt, an agent of any age will have their own GSP. However,  there is a need to limit the range of age to 18 years in order to establish coherent set of rules for further analysis of these emotional intelligent agents.  

Definition of “God Divine agent”- is an autonomous intelligent agent, given own GSP (goal, standards, and preferences),  capable of achieving some goal by appraising internal states derived by “parent” and “child” agents and result in some action taken (invoke some emotion).

Depending on past dispositions, the agent appraises their world and perhaps delves in a state of disillusionment, thus confirming their beliefs towards agent (God).

In appraising the prospective agents for this study, content theory of emotions involves analyzing discrete time and in this study,  basic analysis of elicit emotional responses will be the focus on hand, particularly, analyzing relationship mood variables.

The consensus is formed by evaluating personalities through personal learning experience and understanding/formulating meaning behind stories heard from many people. For example, a parent exhibits a personality of strictness toward child agent, as time passes, child agent manifests emotions of anger, the anger can intensify or possibly be subdued as times passes. Basic research arguments

    The subject of love is very subjective in terms of meaning and understanding, this intrinsic quality is manifested through actions and verbal cues. Depending on the domain and emotionally intelligent agents involved, each state of agent derives some “personality” according to how the agent perceives its world, actions are processed in form of emotions.  

     Appraising an agent’s internal state must be analyzed to deem noteworthy, “Appraisals are cognitive, although not necessarily conscious. They are signals that set body and mind into modes that have been shaped by evolution and individual experience to prompt a person toward certain kinds of action appropriate to the generic event and to impart urgency to these actions.” (Oatley, 2014)

        Appraisal of agent’s environment may be a bit construed dealing with multiple agents. For example, the parent agent possess certain personality/personalities (doting, strict or authoritarian, or some combination) and according to their GSP, their emotional perception towards their child is “love”. Depending on child’s GSP and how they view the world in reference to parents, different emotions are elicited. To illustrate, Mary (agent)  is viewed to be a doting parent by child Joe, and parent Sally is viewed to be strict by child Mike. Nevertheless, both agents, Mary and Sally perceive emotion of  “love” for their children in reference to their respective GSP.  Another view point, parent Sally can change her disposition, her personality to “doting” from “strict”, thus eliciting another set of emotion (from  anger, bitterness ->happy, content, admiration). It is the different styles of personalities that lends itself to the notion of “degrees of love”. This interdependency of emotions involving intelligent agents causes complex connections and particularly seen in multi agent systems.

   Reading: Using an Emotional Intelligent Agent to Improve the Learner’s    

                   Performance

   The title of this article caught my eye and very interested reading in more depth. In fact, the main ideas of this article provided ideas for use of my content theory. The authors, Chaffar and Frasson does a well job illustrating the architecture of emotionally intelligent agents (EIA) includes three modules: perception, control and action. The perception module observes the current state of the agent and its environment and uses a decision tree to store colors of emotions representing the feelings of the agent. The next module, control module perceives state of agent and determines if there is a relationship between agent’s GSP and event. A function evaluates the state to check if state is positive, negative or zero. This result is used for analysis in next module, action, where an emotion is elicited. This is accomplished by asking general questions, from this learning is performed, inferences made and features generated in form of personalities. For example, if agent has personality of “extrovert”, then emotion elicited would be joy.

     Link: https://www.iro.umontreal.ca/~its/art/chaffar_frasson_2004b.pdf
